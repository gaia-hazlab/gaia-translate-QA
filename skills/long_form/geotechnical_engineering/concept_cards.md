# Geotechnical engineering — concept cards

Each card defines one core geotechnical variable, equation, or measured quantity, with units, numerical ranges, and explicit hooks into the other Gaia disciplines. Format follows `docs/card_format_spec.md`.

## CC-geotech-MohrCoulomb: Effective stress σ' and Mohr-Coulomb shear strength τ_f [kPa, MPa]

**Quantity**: effective stress σ' = σ − p (Terzaghi 1925) is the inter-granular stress that controls soil shear strength. The Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion τ_f = c' + σ' tan φ' gives the shear stress at failure on a plane, parameterized by drained cohesion c' [kPa] and effective friction angle φ' [°]. SI units Pa; conventionally kPa for shallow soils, MPa for crustal rock.

**Defining relation**: τ_f = c' + (σ_n − p) tan φ'. On a fault plane the same equation appears as the Coulomb failure criterion of seismology (`CC-seismo-stress`). For cohesionless soils c' ≈ 0; for overconsolidated clays c' = 5–50 kPa.

**Typical ranges**:
- φ' loose sand: 28–32°; dense sand: 36–42°; gravel: 38–45°.
- φ' clay (drained): 18–28°.
- c' normally consolidated clay: ≈ 0; stiff overconsolidated clay: 10–50 kPa.
- σ'_v0 at 10 m depth, hydrostatic: ≈ 100 kPa.

**Cross-discipline equivalents**:
- **Seismology**: identical equation governs fault slip (`CC-seismo-stress`). The bridge `TC-02` is the single most-cited in the corpus.
- **Hydrology**: p is the same pore pressure as `CC-hydro-p`; σ' couples to head changes via Terzaghi-Biot poroelasticity.
- **Geomorphology**: Iverson (2000) transient pore pressure model is Mohr-Coulomb on a hillslope (`PD-AR-landslide`).

**When you see this in a paper**: confirm drained vs. undrained framework (effective vs. total stress); φ' from drained triaxial vs. apparent φ from total-stress analysis can differ by 5–15°.

**Anchor citations**:
- Terzaghi, K. (1943). *Theoretical Soil Mechanics*. Wiley, New York.
- Bishop, A. W. (1959). The principle of effective stress. *Teknisk Ukeblad*, 106(39), 859–863.
- Lambe, T. W., & Whitman, R. V. (1969). *Soil Mechanics*. Wiley, New York.

**Related cards**: `CC-hydro-p`, `CC-seismo-stress`, `CC-geotech-Su`, `CC-geotech-FS`, `TC-02`, `PD-liquefaction`, `PD-AR-landslide`

## CC-geotech-cv: Coefficient of consolidation c_v [m²/s, m²/yr]

**Quantity**: coefficient of consolidation governs the rate at which excess pore pressure dissipates under load. Terzaghi (1925) 1D consolidation: ∂u/∂t = c_v ∂²u/∂z², where u is excess pore pressure and z is depth. c_v = k/(γ_w m_v) — the ratio of hydraulic conductivity to the product of water unit weight γ_w and coefficient of volume compressibility m_v.

**Defining relation**: identical mathematical form to the hydraulic diffusion equation (`CC-hydro-D`), the heat equation, and hillslope sediment-transport diffusion. The shared structure is `TC-01`.

**Typical ranges**:
- Soft clay: c_v = 10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁶ m²/s (≈ 0.3–30 m²/yr).
- Stiff clay / silt: 10⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁵ m²/s.
- Sand: 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻² m²/s — drainage essentially instantaneous on engineering timescales.
- Time factor T_v = c_v t / H_d² ; 90% consolidation at T_v ≈ 0.848.

**Cross-discipline equivalents**:
- **Hydrology**: c_v ↔ hydraulic diffusivity D (`CC-hydro-D`). Bridge `TC-01` documents the 10-orders-of-magnitude span.
- **Geomorphology**: hillslope diffusion D ~ 10⁻⁴ m²/yr is the same equation at much slower rates.
- **Seismology**: pore-pressure diffusion controls `PD-induced-seismicity` r²/t cloud expansion.

**When you see this in a paper**: confirm c_v source — oedometer log-time (Casagrande), root-time (Taylor), or back-calculated from in-situ pore-pressure dissipation; methods differ by factor 2–5.

**Anchor citations**:
- Terzaghi, K. (1943). *Theoretical Soil Mechanics*. Wiley.
- Casagrande, A., & Fadum, R. E. (1940). Notes on soil testing for engineering purposes. *Harvard Soil Mechanics Series*, 8.
- Taylor, D. W. (1948). *Fundamentals of Soil Mechanics*. Wiley.

**Related cards**: `CC-hydro-D`, `CC-hydro-K`, `TC-01`, `PD-subsidence-consolidation`, `PD-induced-seismicity`

## CC-geotech-PorePressure: Pore-pressure response and Skempton parameters [kPa]

**Quantity**: excess pore pressure Δu generated by changes in total stress under undrained loading, parameterized by Skempton (1954) A and B coefficients: Δu = B[Δσ_3 + A(Δσ_1 − Δσ_3)]. The pore-pressure ratio rᵤ = Δu/σ'_v0 is the canonical liquefaction observable.

**Defining relation**: under saturation B ≈ 1; A varies with stress history (loose/normally-consolidated: A = 0.5–1.5; dense/overconsolidated: A = −0.5 to 0.3). Skempton parameters link mechanical loading to the pore-pressure forcing that drives effective-stress changes in `CC-geotech-MohrCoulomb`.

**Typical ranges**:
- B: 0.95–1.00 for fully saturated soil; falls rapidly below ≈ 99% saturation.
- A at failure: 0.7–1.3 loose sand; −0.2–0.2 dense sand; 0.5–1.0 normally consolidated clay.
- Liquefaction onset: rᵤ → 1 (σ' → 0).

**Cross-discipline equivalents**:
- **Hydrology**: pore pressure p is the same quantity as `CC-hydro-p`; under transient undrained loading Δu adds to the static field.
- **Seismology**: induced-seismicity pressure perturbations propagate by diffusion (`PD-induced-seismicity`); the failure trigger is the same effective-stress reduction.
- **Geomorphology**: AR-driven pore-pressure transients drive shallow landslides (`PD-AR-landslide`, Iverson 2000).

**When you see this in a paper**: confirm drainage condition (truly undrained requires loading faster than the consolidation timescale of the layer); B-check should be reported for saturated-triaxial work.

**Anchor citations**:
- Skempton, A. W. (1954). The pore-pressure coefficients A and B. *Géotechnique*, 4(4), 143–147. doi:10.1680/geot.1954.4.4.143
- Wang, H. F. (2000). *Theory of Linear Poroelasticity*. Princeton University Press.

**Related cards**: `CC-hydro-p`, `CC-hydro-h`, `CC-geotech-MohrCoulomb`, `CC-geotech-CSR-CRR`, `PD-induced-seismicity`, `PD-liquefaction`, `TC-02`

## CC-geotech-CSR-CRR: Cyclic stress ratio CSR and cyclic resistance ratio CRR [dimensionless]

**Quantity**: liquefaction-triggering observables. CSR = 0.65 (a_max/g)(σ_v0/σ'_v0) r_d is the depth-corrected cyclic shear stress amplitude normalized by initial effective overburden. CRR is the soil-resistance counterpart, evaluated from in-situ N₁,₆₀ (SPT), q_c1N (CPT), or Vs1 (shear-wave velocity). Triggering when CSR ≥ CRR.

**Defining relation**: Seed & Idriss (1971) simplified procedure; modern form Boulanger & Idriss (2014). a_max is peak ground acceleration from `MC-geotech-GMPE`; r_d is the depth reduction factor (≈ 1 at surface, ≈ 0.65 at 12 m).

**Typical ranges**:
- CSR for design events: 0.10–0.40 in the upper 20 m depending on Mw and site amplification.
- CRR ≈ 0.05–0.50 over the range of densities and effective stresses encountered.
- Magnitude scaling factor MSF normalizes CSR to Mw 7.5 reference.

**Cross-discipline equivalents**:
- **Seismology**: a_max ties directly to `CC-seismo-magnitude` via empirical GMPEs (`MC-geotech-GMPE`).
- **Hydrology**: pore pressure rise during cyclic loading (Δu, `CC-geotech-PorePressure`) is the mechanism translating CSR into σ' → 0.
- **Near-surface geophysics**: Vs1 from MASW (`MC-seismo-MASW`) feeds Vs-based triggering curves (Andrus & Stokoe 2000).

**When you see this in a paper**: confirm fines-content correction (Boulanger-Idriss vs. Youd 2001 NCEER curves differ at high fines); MSF and overburden K_σ corrections must be applied at depth.

**Anchor citations**:
- Seed, H. B., & Idriss, I. M. (1971). Simplified procedure for evaluating soil liquefaction potential. *J. Soil Mech. Found. Div. (ASCE)*, 97(9), 1249–1273. doi:10.1061/JSFEAQ.0001662
- Boulanger, R. W., & Idriss, I. M. (2014). CPT and SPT based liquefaction triggering procedures. *Report UCD/CGM-14/01*, UC Davis.
- Andrus, R. D., & Stokoe, K. H. (2000). Liquefaction resistance of soils from shear-wave velocity. *J. Geotech. Geoenviron. Eng.*, 126(11), 1015–1025. doi:10.1061/(ASCE)1090-0241(2000)126:11(1015)

**Related cards**: `CC-geotech-PorePressure`, `CC-seismo-Vs`, `MC-geotech-CPT`, `MC-geotech-SPT`, `PD-liquefaction`, `TC-10`

## CC-geotech-Su: Undrained shear strength of clay [kPa]

**Quantity**: shear strength of saturated fine-grained soil under loading too rapid for drainage. Operates in the total-stress framework: τ_f = Su, the φ_u = 0 condition (Skempton 1948). The short-term failure observable for foundations, embankments, and excavations in clay.

**Defining relation**: total-stress Mohr circle has constant diameter 2 Su at all confining pressures (saturated, undrained). Distinct from effective-stress framework of `CC-geotech-MohrCoulomb`; the two describe different drainage regimes.

**Typical ranges**:
- Very soft clay: Su < 25 kPa.
- Soft clay: 25–50 kPa.
- Medium clay: 50–100 kPa.
- Stiff clay: 100–200 kPa.
- Su/σ'_v0 ratio: 0.20–0.25 for normally consolidated clay (SHANSEP framework, Ladd 1991).

**Cross-discipline equivalents**:
- **Hydrology**: low-permeability clay layers (K from `CC-hydro-K`) keep loading undrained on engineering timescales — the same lithology controls both.
- **Geomorphology**: Su controls deep-seated marine and lacustrine landslides; cohesion in slope stability `CC-geotech-FS` reduces to Su for short-term clay analysis.
- **Geotechnical–seismology**: undrained cyclic strength under earthquake loading; coupled to `CC-geotech-CSR-CRR` for sensitive clays.

**When you see this in a paper**: confirm test method — field vane, CPT (Nkt factor), unconsolidated-undrained triaxial, direct simple shear — methods can differ by 30–50% on the same clay; SHANSEP normalizes to stress history.

**Anchor citations**:
- Skempton, A. W. (1948). The φ = 0 analysis of stability and its theoretical basis. *Proc. 2nd ICSMFE*, Rotterdam, 1, 72–78.
- Ladd, C. C. (1991). Stability evaluation during staged construction. *J. Geotech. Eng. (ASCE)*, 117(4), 540–615. doi:10.1061/(ASCE)0733-9410(1991)117:4(540)

**Related cards**: `CC-geotech-MohrCoulomb`, `CC-geotech-FS`, `MC-geotech-CPT`, `MC-geotech-triaxial`

## CC-geotech-FS: Factor of safety FS [dimensionless]

**Quantity**: factor of safety against slope failure FS = τ_f/τ_d, the ratio of available shear strength to driving shear stress on a candidate failure surface. The engineering deliverable for slope-stability assessment; FS ≥ 1.3 is typical for permanent slopes, FS ≥ 1.5 for dams.

**Defining relation**: limit-equilibrium method searches for the critical surface minimizing FS (Bishop 1955; Janbu 1973; Spencer 1967 frameworks). For an infinite slope on a hillside: FS = (c' + (γ z cos²β − p) tan φ')/(γ z sinβ cosβ) — the same form that governs natural hillslope stability (`TC-11`, Iverson 2000).

**Typical ranges**:
- Stable slope under design conditions: FS = 1.3–2.0.
- Marginally stable, monitored: 1.0 < FS < 1.3.
- Imminent failure: FS → 1.
- Newmark (1965) sliding block: when peak ground acceleration exceeds the yield acceleration a_y at which FS = 1, permanent displacement accumulates.

**Cross-discipline equivalents**:
- **Geomorphology**: identical FS expression for natural hillslopes (Montgomery & Dietrich 1994 SHALSTAB; Iverson 2000); see `TC-11`.
- **Hydrology**: transient p from rainfall infiltration (`CC-hydro-p`, `PD-AR-landslide`) is the trigger that drops FS below 1.
- **Seismology**: a_max-driven Newmark displacement couples `CC-seismo-magnitude` to slope-failure inventories (Jibson 2007).

**When you see this in a paper**: confirm the analysis method (Bishop simplified, Janbu, Morgenstern-Price, finite element); 2D vs. 3D matters for narrow slopes; the drainage assumption (drained c'/φ' vs. undrained Su) determines which strength card applies.

**Anchor citations**:
- Bishop, A. W. (1955). The use of the slip circle in the stability analysis of slopes. *Géotechnique*, 5(1), 7–17. doi:10.1680/geot.1955.5.1.7
- Newmark, N. M. (1965). Effects of earthquakes on dams and embankments. *Géotechnique*, 15(2), 139–160. doi:10.1680/geot.1965.15.2.139
- Duncan, J. M., & Wright, S. G. (2005). *Soil Strength and Slope Stability*. Wiley.

**Related cards**: `CC-geotech-MohrCoulomb`, `CC-geotech-Su`, `PD-coseismic-landslide`, `PD-AR-landslide`, `TC-11`

## CC-geotech-G-Gmax: Modulus reduction G/Gmax and damping ratio ξ(γ) [dimensionless]

**Quantity**: nonlinear soil dynamic stiffness and damping. Small-strain shear modulus Gmax = ρ Vs² (linking directly to `CC-seismo-Vs`). At shear strains γ above ≈ 10⁻⁴, stiffness degrades along a modulus-reduction curve G/Gmax(γ); hysteretic damping ratio ξ(γ) rises from ≈ 1% at small strain to 15–25% near failure.

**Defining relation**: equivalent-linear site response (SHAKE91, Idriss & Sun 1992) iterates linear analyses at strain-compatible (G, ξ); fully nonlinear codes (DEEPSOIL, OpenSees) integrate constitutive laws directly. Curves are soil-type-dependent (Vucetic & Dobry 1991 plasticity-index family; Darendeli 2001).

**Typical ranges**:
- Linear regime: γ < 10⁻⁴ (small-strain seismology, MASW, ambient noise).
- Mild nonlinearity: γ = 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻³; G/Gmax = 0.6–0.95.
- Strong nonlinearity: γ > 10⁻³; G/Gmax < 0.5, ξ > 10%.
- Liquefaction threshold strain ≈ 10⁻⁴ for loose sands.

**Cross-discipline equivalents**:
- **Seismology**: linear-elastic site response (`CC-seismo-Vs`, `MC-seismo-MASW`) is the γ → 0 limit; the depart from linearity is the defining geotechnical contribution (`TC-10`).
- **Near-surface geophysics**: dv/v changes (`CC-seismo-dvv`) at high ground motion partly reflect transient G/Gmax reduction.

**When you see this in a paper**: confirm strain level — small-strain seismology and engineering site response live in different regimes; "Vs profile" alone does not determine ground motion above γ ≈ 10⁻³.

**Anchor citations**:
- Hardin, B. O., & Drnevich, V. P. (1972). Shear modulus and damping in soils: Design equations and curves. *J. Soil Mech. Found. Div. (ASCE)*, 98(7), 667–692.
- Vucetic, M., & Dobry, R. (1991). Effect of soil plasticity on cyclic response. *J. Geotech. Eng. (ASCE)*, 117(1), 89–107. doi:10.1061/(ASCE)0733-9410(1991)117:1(89)
- Darendeli, M. B. (2001). Development of a new family of normalized modulus reduction and material damping curves. Ph.D. dissertation, University of Texas at Austin.

**Related cards**: `CC-seismo-Vs`, `MC-geotech-cyclic-labs`, `MC-geotech-numerical`, `PD-site-response`, `TC-10`, `TC-12`
